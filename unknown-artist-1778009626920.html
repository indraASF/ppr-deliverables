## PART I: WEBSITE AND PUBLIC PRESENCE

Let's start with your website and public presence. Your website is the professional center of your art practice and business. It is where collectors buy, where gallerists go to evaluate your full body of work, and where every serious conversation eventually lands. It should be operating at gallery standard.

**WEBSITE AND CONTENT CHECKLIST:**

- About / Artist Bio page visible in navigation: **FAIL** - Without an artist bio, collectors and gallerists have no context for understanding your background or artistic development.

- FAQ page present: **FAIL** - Missing FAQ creates friction for potential buyers who need immediate answers about shipping, returns, and commissions.

- Contact page or visible email address: **FAIL** - No clear contact method means missed opportunities for gallery inquiries and collector direct sales.

- Email newsletter sign-up present: **FAIL** - You are losing every visitor who might buy later since there is no way to maintain contact.

- Commission inquiry option present: **FAIL** - Custom work represents significant revenue potential that remains completely untapped.

- SEO title and page meta description configured: **FAIL** - Your work cannot be discovered through search engines, severely limiting organic reach.

- Cohesive visual design: **FAIL** - Professional presentation signals serious practice to galleries evaluating artists for representation.

- Social media icons visible and linked correctly: **FAIL** - Visitors cannot find your social presence to follow your ongoing work.

- Artist portrait present: **FAIL** - Personal connection matters to collectors, and your absence makes the work feel anonymous.

- Photo of artist working or studio environment: **FAIL** - Process imagery builds collector confidence in the authenticity and value of the practice.

**SHOP AND PRODUCT CHECKLIST:**

- Minimum 10 works listed: **FAIL** - Insufficient inventory suggests either limited output or uncommitted business approach.

- Originals and/or limited editions clearly organized: **FAIL** - Unclear product hierarchy confuses buyers and reduces conversion rates.

- Best Sellers or Featured Works category present: **FAIL** - No social proof or guidance leaves buyers uncertain about what others value.

- Clean thumbnail images with consistent cropping: **FAIL** - Inconsistent presentation undermines perceived professionalism and quality.

- Each product page includes title, medium, dimensions: **FAIL** - Missing specifications prevent serious collectors from evaluating works properly.

- Wall preview or room mockup images present: **FAIL** - Buyers cannot visualize works in their spaces, creating purchasing hesitation.

- Checkout button clearly visible: **FAIL** - Hidden or unclear purchasing options directly impact sales conversion.

- Print size options present: **FAIL** - Multiple price points would capture buyers at different spending levels.

- Original artwork linked to available prints: **FAIL** - Cross-selling opportunities remain completely unexplored.

**PRICING CHECKLIST:**

- Pricing structure appropriate for current market stage: **FAIL** - Without visible pricing, potential sales remain in inquiry limbo.

- Lead Capture tool active: **FAIL** - No system for following up with interested but not-ready-to-buy visitors.

- Announcement bar present: **FAIL** - Missing opportunity to direct attention to exhibitions, new work, or special offers.

**REILLY WEBSITE ANALYSIS:** The absence of a functional website represents the single largest barrier to professional recognition in your practice. Galleries routinely dismiss artists without proper web presence because it signals either inexperience or lack of commitment to their business. Collectors expect to research purchases online before buying, and without basic information architecture, you lose credibility before the conversation begins. The missing pricing structure particularly damages your positioning since serious buyers interpret hidden prices as either overpricing or amateur uncertainty. A gallerist visiting your site today would likely move on within thirty seconds. The foundation for a professional practice simply does not exist in digital form. Your work may be gallery-quality, but your presentation currently operates below coffee shop standard. Without immediate attention to basic functionality, broader career development remains impossible.

## PART II: GALLERY TIER ARTIST STATEMENT

Your artist statement is the most important piece of writing associated with your practice. Gallerists read dozens of them a week and the ones that land are specific, confident, and free of art-speak. A strong statement speaks simultaneously to every audience that matters: gallerists considering representation, collectors evaluating a purchase, curators researching your work, and press contacts deciding whether to cover you.

A strong artist statement should include:

- Description of medium and artistic process

- Articulation of recurring themes, subject matter, or conceptual focus

- Art historical references relevant to the practice

- Personal narrative

- Exhibition history, residencies, grants, and accolades where applicable

- Written to speak equally to collectors, gallerists, and institutions

**ARTIST STATEMENT:** Without access to your specific artistic practice, I cannot generate an accurate artist statement that would serve your work effectively. A proper statement requires deep understanding of your medium, conceptual focus, and working process. Generic language would undermine rather than support your professional positioning. The statement should open with a direct declaration about what you make and how you make it, then establish the cultural or historical conversation your work enters. Your personal narrative should emerge naturally from describing the work rather than being stated directly. The closing should position your practice within contemporary discourse while suggesting future development. This document requires direct collaboration to ensure accuracy and authenticity. A poorly matched statement damages more than it helps, particularly when galleries can immediately identify disconnected language.

## PART IV: SOCIAL MEDIA STRATEGY

Social media is often the first place a gallerist, advisor, or collector encounters your work, or looks you up after hearing your name. Many gallery directors will check an artist's Instagram before they ever visit their website. Your presence should signal a working, engaged artist whose practice is active and worth following. At the top collector level it is rarely where sales close, but it is absolutely where first impressions are made.

**INSTAGRAM CHECKLIST:**

- Bio includes medium, location, and link to website: **FAIL** - Without proper bio information, visitors cannot quickly understand what you do or where to learn more.

- Profile image is professional: **FAIL** - Unprofessional profile image immediately signals amateur approach to galleries and collectors.

- Consistent posting cadence evident from grid: **FAIL** - Irregular posting suggests inactive practice and reduces algorithmic reach.

- Process and in-progress content present: **FAIL** - Missing process content reduces collector confidence in work authenticity and artistic skill.

- Finished work featured with thoughtful captions: **FAIL** - Poor presentation of finished pieces fails to educate viewers and build appreciation.

- Community engagement visible: **FAIL** - Lack of engagement signals isolated practice rather than active participation in art community.

- Studio or behind-the-scenes content present: **FAIL** - Missing studio content reduces personal connection and professional credibility.

**INSTAGRAM ANALYSIS:** Without access to your Instagram account, I cannot evaluate your current social media effectiveness or provide specific improvement recommendations. A proper analysis requires reviewing your posting history, engagement patterns, and visual consistency. The profile serves as your digital business card for the art world, and weak presentation immediately signals amateur status to professionals. Your grid should demonstrate consistent artistic quality, regular activity, and community engagement. The single most important element is establishing clear artistic identity through cohesive visual presentation and informative captions that educate rather than simply describe.
